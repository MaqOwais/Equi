# Equi — Competitor Analysis & Unique Selling Points

All competitor assessments are based on publicly available app store listings, user reviews, and published clinical literature on digital mental health tools as of 2025.

---

## Competitor Landscape

### Direct Competitors — Bipolar-Specific Apps

#### eMoods Bipolar Mood Tracker
The most established bipolar-specific tracker on the market. Available on iOS and Android. Free with a premium tier.

**What it does well:**
- Dedicated mania, depression, mixed, and irritability tracking
- Medication logging
- Generates shareable PDF reports for psychiatrists
- Long track record and trusted by clinicians

**Where it falls short:**
- No AI analysis — reports are charts, not interpreted insights
- No community features
- No wearable integration
- No crisis mode
- No prescribed activity system or psychiatrist marketplace
- No social rhythm tracking or IPSRT-based tools
- No relapse signature or personalised early warning system
- Dated UI — no block-based journal

---

#### Bearable
A comprehensive symptom tracker positioning itself as suitable for any chronic condition, not bipolar-specific.

**What it does well:**
- Highly customisable — users define their own symptoms, factors, treatments
- Wearable integration (Apple Health, Google Fit)
- Correlation charts across logged factors
- Export to CSV and PDF

**Where it falls short:**
- Not clinically grounded — no evidence base for feature design choices
- No AI interpretation — correlation charts require the user to draw their own conclusions
- No psychiatrist integration or prescribed activities
- No community
- No crisis mode
- Not bipolar-specific — lacks cycle-phase awareness, IPSRT tools, relapse signatures
- No moderation-safe community
- Subscription model; data practices not prominently disclosed

---

#### Optimism (Black Dog Institute)
A mood tracker developed by an Australian clinical research institute. Available free on iOS and Android.

**What it does well:**
- Clinically developed — backed by a leading bipolar and depression research institute
- Wellness plan feature — users document their own warning signs and triggers
- Covers both bipolar and depression

**Where it falls short:**
- No AI — purely manual tracking with charts
- Minimal wearable integration
- No community
- No psychiatrist marketplace
- No prescribed activities
- UI is functional but minimal — not built for daily engagement
- Australia-focused; limited US psychiatrist pathway

---

### Indirect Competitors — General Mood & Wellness Apps

#### Daylio
A micro-journal and mood tracker with a large user base. No clinical framing.

**What it does well:**
- Very low friction — quick daily mood + activity log in under 30 seconds
- Clean, engaging UI
- Streaks and statistics
- Large established user base

**Where it falls short:**
- No bipolar-specific design — single mood scale, not mania/depressive/mixed states
- No clinical grounding whatsoever
- Gamification (streaks) can be harmful for users in depressive episodes who miss days
- No AI insights
- No community, no crisis mode, no psychiatrist pathway
- No wearable integration

---

#### Woebot
An AI chatbot for mental health support, CBT-based. FDA Breakthrough Device Designation.

**What it does well:**
- Conversational AI that feels approachable
- Evidence-based CBT techniques
- FDA breakthrough device — clinical credibility
- No human therapist required

**Where it falls short:**
- Not designed for bipolar disorder — CBT without anti-hypomania guardrails can be contraindicated
- No monitoring or tracking — purely conversational
- No wearable integration
- No psychiatrist pathway
- No community
- Closed model; data practices less transparent

---

#### BetterHelp / Talkspace
Teletherapy platforms connecting users to licensed therapists and psychiatrists.

**What they do well:**
- Access to real licensed therapists and psychiatrists
- Async messaging + video sessions
- Large provider networks

**Where they fall short:**
- No monitoring between sessions — therapist only sees what the user reports verbally
- No AI-generated reports — no data continuity
- No community
- No crisis mode
- Subscription cost is high ($260–$400/month)
- Multiple controversies around data privacy and provider quality
- Not bipolar-specific

---

#### Headspace / Calm
General wellness and meditation apps.

**What they do well:**
- High-quality audio content and sleep tools
- Strong brand recognition
- Large content libraries

**Where they fall short:**
- Entirely generic — no clinical grounding for bipolar
- Mindfulness without anti-hypomania guardrails is contraindicated during manic phases (documented in clinical literature)
- No tracking, no AI insights, no psychiatrist pathway
- Subscription model; primarily content businesses

## Equi's Unique Selling Points

### 1. The only bipolar app designed entirely from clinical evidence
Every feature in Equi is tied to peer-reviewed research — CANMAT 2023 guidelines, IPSRT landmark RCTs, Cochrane reviews, ISBD meta-analyses. This isn't a wellness app that added a mood tracker. The features exist because the research says they should exist. Full evidence base documented in [`evidence-review.md`](evidence-review.md).

### 2. AI that speaks to your psychiatrist
No competitor generates AI-narrated, structured wellness reports that correlate sleep, mood, journal tone, medication adherence, and social rhythm into a readable clinical document. Equi's reports are designed to be shared before appointments — giving psychiatrists real longitudinal data instead of a 10-minute verbal summary from memory.

### 3. Personalised Relapse Signature — monitored by AI
Research shows that each person's early warning signs are unique and appear 2–4 weeks before a full episode (Cochrane-reviewed). Equi is the first consumer app to build a structured Relapse Signature Builder and have the AI actively cross-reference it against live data. No other app in this space does this.

### 4. Social Rhythm Tracking (IPSRT)
The Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy framework — one of the strongest behavioural targets in bipolar disorder (landmark Frank et al. RCTs) — is not implemented in any consumer bipolar app. Equi's five-anchor daily logging and Rhythm Consistency Score brings an evidence-based clinical tool directly to the user's phone.

### 5. Safety-first design — the only app with built-in clinical risk mitigations
Digital mental health apps for bipolar disorder carry documented clinical risks: rumination from mood monitoring, hypomania induction from positive activities, contraindicated mindfulness during manic phases. Equi is the only app that has read the safety literature and built specific mitigations into the design — phase-aware activity gating, low-mood safety circuits, honest evidence labelling. Full details in [`safety-flags.md`](safety-flags.md).

### 6. Prescribed Activities — a direct psychiatrist-to-app workflow
Psychiatrists can prescribe specific therapeutic activities with dosage and frequency directly through the Equi portal. This creates a closed loop: psychiatrist prescribes, patient completes in-app, compliance data feeds back to psychiatrist. No consumer mental health app offers this workflow.

### 7. Donation-funded — the only mental health app where your data is never the product
Equi is funded by donations that directly sponsor access for users who cannot afford care. No subscription, no ads, no data sold. A transparent spending dashboard shows exactly where every dollar goes. This is a structural differentiator — not a marketing claim.

### 8. Community built for the population, not for engagement metrics
No algorithmic feed, no likes, no streaks. Only *"I relate"* and *"Thank you for sharing"*. Anonymous by default. Crisis hotline pinned at the top. Every post is AI-moderated before going live, with transparent flagging — not silent removal. Built for people in genuine distress, not for time-on-app.

### 9. Substance use monitoring — the gap no competitor fills
No other bipolar app tracks alcohol or cannabis use alongside mood and sleep — despite both being documented cycle destabilisers (MinDag 2025, Lindner Center). Equi adds a frictionless two-tap daily check-in with non-judgmental framing, feeding patterns into the AI report without shaming or moralising.

### 10. Open source — community accountability over clinical claims
Equi's codebase is open for review. Anyone — including clinicians, researchers, and people with lived experience — can audit the safety mitigations, the AI prompts, the data handling. This matters for a clinical-grade tool in a population with high stigma and low institutional trust.

## Market Gap Summary

The gap Equi fills is specific: **a bipolar-specialist app that is clinically rigorous, AI-powered, safety-aware, and free at the point of use.**

- eMoods is the closest competitor but has no AI and is a decade behind in design
- General wellness apps (Headspace, Calm, Daylio) are actively contraindicated for some bipolar users without phase-aware guardrails
- Teletherapy platforms (BetterHelp) provide human access but no monitoring continuity
- No app combines: research-backed features + AI reporting + prescribed activities + psychiatrist integration + social rhythm tracking + relapse signature monitoring + substance use monitoring + safety-first design + donation funding

That combination is the gap. That is Equi.
